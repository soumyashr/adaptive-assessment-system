What is Item Response Theory?

Mathematical Framework for Ability Measurement

Item Response Theory (IRT) models the relationship between a student's ability and their probability of answering questions correctly. This enables precise measurement with fewer questions through adaptive testing.

3-Parameter Logistic (3PL) Model

P(θ) = c + (1-c) / (1 + e-a(θ-b))

θ (theta): Student ability level (estimated in real-time)

a: Discrimination parameter (how well the question differentiates ability)

b: Difficulty parameter (ability level needed for 50% success probability)

c: Guessing parameter (probability of correct guess ≈ 0.25)

References: Lord (1980), Embretson & Reise (2000)

Question Matching Process

  • Initial θ is mapped to a competency tier (C1, C2, C3, or C4)
  • Questions are filtered by tier-appropriate difficulty ranges
  • Fisher Information is calculated for each eligible question at θ₀
  • The question with maximum information is selected to optimize measurement precision
Example: An intermediate student (θ = 0.0) is assigned to Tier C2, which contains questions with difficulty parameters ranging from -1.0 to +1.0.

References: van der Linden & Glas (2010)

Adaptive Question Selection

Multi-Stage Selection Algorithm

Theta Update (Newton-Raphson Method)

Recalculate ability estimate based on all previous responses using maximum likelihood estimation

Anticipated Theta Calculation

Predict future ability using recent response patterns (1.5× adjustment multiplier for proactive selection)

Fisher Information Maximization

Calculate information value for each eligible question at the anticipated ability level

Constraint Application

Apply business rules: no question repetition, difficulty progression after correct responses, content balancing, exposure control

Fisher Information Formula

I(θ) = a² × p*(1-p*) / (1-c)²

Higher information = more precise ability measurement at that theta level

References: Chang & Ying (1996), Wang et al. (2023)

Ability Estimation Algorithm

Newton-Raphson Maximum Likelihood Estimation

Iterative Convergence Method

θn+1 = θn - L'(θ) / L''(θ)

L'(θ): First derivative of log-likelihood function

L''(θ): Second derivative (equivalent to Fisher Information)

Convergence: Typically achieves stable estimates within 5-10 iterations

Conservative Update Limits

Early Questions (1-5): Maximum change ±0.2

Later Questions (6+): Maximum change ±0.3

Prevents extreme oscillations and ensures measurement stability

Exponential Smoothing

θsmooth = α × θnew + (1-α) × θcurrent

Smoothing factor (α) ranges from 0.3 to 0.7, balancing responsiveness with stability

References: Baker & Kim (2004), Bock & Mislevy (1982)

Assessment Termination Criteria

Multi-Criteria Stopping Rules

1. Standard Error of Measurement (Primary Criterion)

SEM = 1 / √(Σ Information)

Target SEM: ≤ 0.25 for diagnostic testing, ≤ 0.35 for placement testing
Interpretation: Lower SEM indicates higher precision in ability estimation

2. Question Limits

Minimum: 10 questions

Maximum: 30 questions

Ensures sufficient data while preventing excessive test length

3. Theta Stability

Variance of last 5 estimates < 0.01

Indicates convergence to stable ability estimate

4. Consecutive Response Patterns

Six or more consecutive correct/incorrect responses indicate ceiling or floor effects, suggesting the test has reached the limits of measurement precision for that ability level.

95% Confidence Interval: θ ± 1.96 × SEM

References: Kingsbury & Zara (1989), Weiss (1982)
